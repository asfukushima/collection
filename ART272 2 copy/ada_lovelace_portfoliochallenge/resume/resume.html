Ada Lovelace

Born: 1815
Location: London, England

Key Events in Ada Lovelace's Life

  • 1819: From the age of 4, Lovelace was tutored in mathematics and science, an unusual course of study for a woman in 19th-century England.
  • 1827: At the age of 12, Lovelace conceptualized a flying machine.
  • 1843: Lovelace translated into English an article written by military engineer—and future Italian prime minister—Luigi Menabrea about Charles Babbage’s theoretical Analytical Engine.
  • 1845: Lovelace foresaw the multi-purpose functionality of the modern computer. She mused that any piece of content—including music, text, pictures, and sounds—could be translated to digital form and manipulated by machine.
  • 1847: Lovelace gained gambling "experience" and developed a habit that contributed to her dwindling finances.
  • 1852: Charles Dickens read a passage from one of his novels to Lovelace on her deathbed. Three months later, Lovelace passed away on November 27, 1852.
  • 1953: Lovelace's notes on Babbage’s Analytical Engine are republished in B.V. Bowden’s book, “Faster Than Thought: A Symposium on Digital Computing Machines.”
  • 1970: A computer programming language is named in Lovelace’s honor. The Ada language is still used globally in the operation of real-time systems in aviation, health care, transportation, financial, infrastructure, and space industries.
